Problem · the limits of current AGV navigation technologies

Why don't AGVs sell? A few real barriers.

High deployment cost

Traditional 2D systems (LiDAR, encoders, odometry) require expensive components and heavy IT infrastructure. 3D navigation on classic linear LiDARs is extremely costly — a single scanner costing >USD 5,000 drives up the per-unit price of the robot and limits mass automation.

Losing orientation in changing environments

2D navigation (SLAM) loses orientation when the layout of the hall changes, when obstacles or racks shift → localization errors and manual relocalization. Even classic 3D (ICP, FastLIO2) running on odometry and feature points is unstable when the environment changes.

Odometry and wheel slip (position drift)

Encoder-based systems are prone to slip → position errors on the map (drift) and the need for operator intervention. The robots move "blindly" → growing collision risk.

Today the main barrier to AGV adoption is the PRICE per robot — old, expensive technology is holding the market back.
Our solution makes AGVs accessible to most small and mid-sized factories — not just the big corporations.

How it works

The robot builds and reads a 3D map in real time.

  • Livox Mid360 LiDAR — a spherical 360° pattern that covers 99% of the surroundings in 1 second (a full 3D model even when stationary).
  • LiDAR + IMU fusion — the IMU acts as an internal compass; orientation comes from a Madgwick filter.
  • 100 Hz — a position update every <10 ms, continuous relocalization while driving up to 2 m/s.
  • The map updates itself — with no prior "learning" of the space.

Localization algorithm · the heart of the technology

Four decisions that create the edge.

  • ① Feature-less SLAM — it overlays the entire dense point cloud directly onto the map (scan-to-map), with no markers → resilience to scene changes.
  • ② NDT instead of "point-to-point" — a grid of cells described by a normal distribution; matching = maximizing the likelihood. Faster than ICP.
  • ③ Fusion with a motion model + IMU — the result is always bounded and stable (something Fast-LIO2 does not guarantee); no drift from wheel slip.
  • ④ 1 CPU core · zero GPU — analytical Jacobians (SymPy) on Eigen, <10 ms per update → a drastically lower cost.

The complete transport of raw material and finished goods — with no human involvement.

We have already solved localization. Our latest invention goes further: goods flow from delivery to dispatch without human hands. We invert the logic of the warehouse — the bin is at the center, and the rack, the AGV, and the software work around it.

  • Proprietary bins — our own family (from a small box to a pallet container), designed for smooth flow.
  • Non-humanoid robots — the AGVs automatically load and unload boxes and bins. No human touches the goods.
